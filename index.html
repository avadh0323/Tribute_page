NARENDRA DAMODARDAS MODI

Journey From Tea Seller To Prime Minister

Narendra Modi, the 14th Prime Minister of India, has had a remarkable journey that has captivated the nation and the world. Born on September 17, 1950, in Vadnagar, Gujarat, Modi’s rise to power is a testament to his determination, leadership, and unwavering commitment to serving the people of India. Modi’s early life was marked by humble beginnings. He came from a modest family and worked as a tea seller in his hometown. Despite the challenges he faced, Modi never let his circumstances define him. He pursued his education and completed a degree in political science. Modi’s entry into politics came in the early 1980s when he joined the Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh (RSS), a Hindu nationalist organization. His dedication and hard work quickly caught the attention of his superiors, and he rose through the ranks of the organization. In 2001, Modi became the Chief Minister of Gujarat, a position he held for an unprecedented four terms. During his tenure, Gujarat witnessed significant economic growth and development. Modi’s leadership and business-friendly policies earned him praise and recognition both within India and internationally. In 2014, Modi’s journey took a historic turn when he was elected as the Prime Minister of India. His campaign, centered around the promise of economic reforms and development, struck a chord with the Indian electorate. Modi’s party, the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP), secured a landslide victory, and he became the first Prime Minister in three decades to secure a majority in the Indian Parliament. As Prime Minister, Modi has implemented a series of transformative reforms aimed at improving the lives of ordinary Indians. His flagship initiatives, such as Swachh Bharat Abhiyan (Clean India Mission), Digital India, and Make in India, have been instrumental in driving economic growth, promoting digital inclusion, and attracting foreign investment. Narendra Modi’s journey from a tea seller to the Prime Minister of India is a testament to the power of determination and hard work. His rise to power has inspired millions of Indians and serves as a reminder that anyone, regardless of their background, can achieve greatness.


START OF MODI ERA IN INDIA

As Prime Minister Modi's government celebrates its 8 year anniversary, it is pertinent to trace the journey that began in 2014 to see how it has redefined the growth story of one of the largest nations in the world and given rise to a governance model focused on welfare, sustainability and prosperity for all. Below are the 8 major achievements of the Modi government that have defined his governance approach and has contributed enormously to India's growth while setting the stage for the country to emerge as a major power in the years to come.Propelling Entrepreneurship: Make in India and StartUp India Make in India, launched in 2014, was one of the first initiatives to facilitate investment, foster innovation, build best-in-class infrastructure, and make India a hub for manufacturing, design, and innovation. Under this program, India has consistently received record FDI inflows and jumped several places in many global indices and rankings such as Global Innovation Index. Startup India which has been at the forefront of efforts to build a strong eco-system for nurturing innovation and startups in the country to drive sustainable economic growth and generate large scale employment opportunities has been scaling new heights each year. Benefits to startups under this initiative include fast tracking and rebates on applications for intellectual property, access to Rs. 10,000 crore worth of Fund of Funds, self-certification under labour and environmental laws, income tax exemptions for 3 years among many others.Through this program, India’s climate action strategies aim for clean and efficient energy systems, disaster resilient infrastructure, and planned eco-restoration. India has a SDG vertical within Niti Aayog as the nodal agency for monitoring the Sustainable Development Goals and cooperating through different global mechanisms. India has electrified 100% of its villages, reduced 38 million tonnes of CO2 emissions annually through energy efficient appliances, provided clean cooking fuel to 80 million poor households, and set a target to install 450 GW of renewable energy and restore 26 million hectares of degraded land by 2030. Globally, India stands third in renewable power, fourth in wind power, and fifth in solar power. India launched the Coalition for Disaster Resilient Infrastructure and the International Solar Alliance to leverage global partnerships for climate action and disaster resilience.

DEVELOPMENT OF INDIA UNDER BJP RULE

India has become one of the fastest developing countries in the world under the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) rule The changed economic policies introduced by the BJP government at the Centre has taken the country on a different path in the economic sector and therefore, it has seen wide, digital growth. The Congress, which had ruled the country for over five decades, ‘donates’ poverty to the people of this nation as it did not launch any concrete economic policies to help develop the country. But, when the BJP came to power, it first auctioned 65 coal mines and collected a revenue of ₹3.24 lakh crore which was then released to the States where coal mines are located Economic growth increase in per capita Gross Domestic Product, or GDP per capita is one of the indicators of economic development. Other things constant, a situation of rising GDP per capita is, better for common people than one where it is not rising. There is little to suggest that economic growth has been stellar during the 2014-2017 period. If anything, the numbers have declined. This is thanks to not only the regressive GST which has hurt small-scale producers, but also to demonetization the logic of which has not yet been clear. It should be noted that the government has kept on changing its own explanation of its policy of demon-etization from eliminating corruption to terror funding, to formalization of economic transactions by reducing cash use, etc., and that the failure of the policy has not been admitted by the government. Could it be that: this policy was introduced on behalf of, and at the behest of, a segment of the capitalist class that deals in e-technologies or e-transactions, but was justified in terms of the benefits for all.
